Planet Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest one to the Sun. Because it lacks a substantial atmosphere to trap heat, it experiences extreme temperature swings, ranging from scorching hot during the day to freezing cold at night. Its surface is rocky and covered with thousands of craters, making it look very similar to our Moon. Despite its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is actually the second hottest planet after Venus, and it speeds around the Sun faster than any other planet, completing a full orbit in just 88 Earth days.

Planet Venus

Venus is often called Earth’s "twin" because they are similar in size and structure, but it is actually the hottest planet in our solar system. Its thick atmosphere is mostly made of carbon dioxide, which traps heat like a greenhouse, raising surface temperatures to a scorching 475 degrees Celsius (900 degrees Fahrenheit). Interestingly, Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most other planets, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. Its surface is a landscape of mountains and thousands of volcanoes, all hidden beneath thick, yellowish clouds of sulfuric acid

Planet Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only place in the universe known to harbor life. It is often called the "Blue Planet" because liquid water covers about 70% of its surface, which is essential for all living organisms. Earth has a protective atmosphere rich in nitrogen and oxygen that shields us from harmful solar radiation and maintains a stable temperature. Unlike its neighbors, Earth is a dynamic world with shifting tectonic plates, active volcanoes, and diverse ecosystems ranging from frozen poles to tropical rainforests.

Planet Mars

Mars is a cold, desert world often called the Red Planet because of the iron-rich dust that covers its surface. It has a very thin atmosphere, mostly made of carbon dioxide, and is home to the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. Although it is much smaller than Earth, a day on Mars lasts about 24.6 hours, making it feel somewhat familiar. Today, scientists study Mars with robotic rovers to find out if it ever had water or signs of ancient life in the past.
